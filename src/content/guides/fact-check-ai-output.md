The most dangerous thing about AI isn't that it's sometimes wrong. It's that it is wrong *in exactly the same confident tone* it uses when it's right. There's no stammer to listen for. If you publish AI-assisted content — videos, posts, product pages, client work — you need a checking habit. Here's mine.

## Sort claims by blast radius

Not everything needs checking. "The sky appears blue due to light scattering" — fine. But three categories get verified every time:

1. **Numbers** — prices, percentages, dates, statistics. AI mixes up years and invents plausible figures constantly.
2. **Anything time-sensitive** — model names, tool features, platform rules, what something costs. AI's knowledge has a cutoff date; the world doesn't.
3. **Anything with consequences** — health, money, legal, safety. Wrong here doesn't cost views, it costs trust or worse.

## A snippet is not a source

When you search to verify, don't stop at the search-result preview text — it's often AI-generated too, or quoting the same wrong article. Open the actual page. Find the original source: the company's own pricing page, the study itself, the official documentation. The test I use: *have I actually seen the primary source, or have I only seen someone describing it?*

## Ask the AI to cite, then check the citations

Asking "what's your source?" does two useful things. Sometimes the AI admits it's unsure — valuable. Sometimes it names a source — which you then open, because AI also invents citations that sound real, right down to plausible dates.

## Label what you couldn't verify

Sometimes you can't confirm a claim and want to use it anyway. Say so: "reportedly," "as of early 2026," "I couldn't verify this." Readers forgive honest uncertainty. They don't forgive confident nonsense.

## Make it a checklist, not a virtue

The failure mode is believing you'll "be careful." You won't, at 11pm, on video #40. What works is mechanical: before anything publishes, list the factual claims, mark each ✓ verified / ✗ removed / ⚠ labeled. Five minutes. I've had entire scripts survive review untouched, and I've had ones where three "facts" died at the checkpoint — those three were the ones that would have been in the comments forever.
